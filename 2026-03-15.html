41,000-Year-Old Neanderthal Bones Reveal Systematic Cannibalism — and It Wasn’t a Last Resort
New analysis of Neanderthal bones from a Belgian cave (Goyet) has confirmed a disturbing pattern: signs of de-fleshing, marrow extraction, and bone fragmentation consistent with cannibalism — not as a survival behaviour, but as deliberate exocannibalism targeting members of a rival group. Women and juveniles appear to have been specifically targeted. Researchers now believe inter-group violence and predatory cannibalism was more common in Neanderthal society than previously thought.
The dinner-table version: Turns out Neanderthals weren’t just competing with humans for territory — they were actively hunting each other. Makes the school run feel manageable.
