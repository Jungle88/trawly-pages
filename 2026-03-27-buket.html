🔥 The Big One
COMPASS: Pan-Cancer AI Model Predicts Immunotherapy Response Across 7 Cancer Types
A new foundation model called COMPASS uses a concept-bottleneck transformer trained on 10,184 tumours across 33 cancer types to predict immunotherapy response from bulk tumour transcriptomes. Validated across 16 independent clinical cohorts spanning seven cancers and six checkpoint inhibitors, it outperforms 22 baseline methods — increasing accuracy by 8.5% and area under the precision-recall curve by 15.7%. COMPASS-stratified responders show significantly longer overall survival (HR = 4.7, p < 0.0001). The model encodes gene expression through 44 biologically grounded immune concepts representing immune cell states, TME interactions, and signalling pathways.
Why it matters: This is the first generalizable AI model that transfers immunotherapy predictions to unseen cancer types and drugs without retraining. It could fundamentally change how clinical trials are designed — enabling patient stratification before enrolment rather than after failure.
🎯 Why this matters to you As a hospital scientist in cancer genetics, COMPASS represents the kind of tool that could land in your clinical workflow within a few years. The concept-bottleneck architecture means it’s interpretable — it maps back to immune concepts you already work with (TGF-β signalling, CD4+ T cell dysfunction, B cell deficiency). The preprint is on medRxiv now with a live demo at immuno-compass.com.

🧬 Cancer Genetics & AI
METASTASIS PREDICTION
MangroveGS: New AI Tool Predicts Cancer Spread from Gene Expression Patterns
University of Geneva researchers identified gene expression signatures in colon cancer cells that determine whether a tumour will metastasise. Their AI model, MangroveGS, converts these genetic signals into predictions that generalise across multiple cancer types. The study frames cancer as “a distorted form of development” — where reactivated embryonic programs drive metastatic potential rather than random mutations.
Why it matters: This reframes metastasis from “anarchic cells” to structured developmental biology gone wrong — opening new therapeutic targets. MangroveGS could enable clinicians to estimate metastatic risk from tumour biopsy alone.
🎯 Why this matters to you The developmental biology angle here maps directly to the kind of chromosomal and epigenetic patterns you see in cytogenetics. If metastasis is driven by reactivated developmental programs, those signals should be visible at the karyotype and FISH level too. Published in Cell Reports — worth reading the full methods.

IMMUNOTHERAPY
Decoding the Tumour: AI Maps Molecular Conversations Inside Colorectal Cancers
Karin Pelka at the Gladstone Institutes is using spatial transcriptomics and AI to identify “central regulators” — master molecular switches that control whether cancer cells are visible to the immune system. Her mission: extend immunotherapy’s extraordinary results from the 5–10% of colorectal patients with dMMR tumours to the other 90%. The 2022 NEJM trial showing 100% response in dMMR rectal cancer remains the benchmark she’s trying to generalise.
Why it matters: Colorectal cancer is now the leading cause of cancer death in adults under 50. If Pelka’s spatial transcriptomics approach can surface new immunotherapy targets that work across genetically diverse tumours, it could transform treatment for the majority of patients who currently don’t respond.

HRT + WEIGHT LOSS
Mayo Clinic: HRT + Tirzepatide Produces 35% Greater Weight Loss in Postmenopausal Women
A Mayo Clinic-led study published in The Lancet Obstetrics, Gynaecology & Women’s Health reports that postmenopausal women using menopausal hormone therapy alongside tirzepatide lost approximately 35% more weight than those on tirzepatide alone. The observational study analysed 120 adults treated for at least 12 months. The findings point to a synergy between estrogen replacement and GLP-1/GIP receptor agonists that hasn’t been well characterised until now.
Why it matters: This is the first data showing HRT may significantly enhance GLP-1 drug efficacy. With menopause-associated weight gain driving cardiovascular risk, combined therapy could become a new standard of care for cardiometabolic management in postmenopausal women.
🎯 Why this matters to you You’re in early perimenopause awareness while working full-time and raising two boys. This Lancet paper adds important nuance to the HRT decision — the benefits may extend well beyond symptom relief into metabolic health and weight management. Worth filing for future conversations with your GP.

HRT & COGNITION
HRT Started Around Menopause Unlikely to Increase Dementia Risk
New research measuring plasma phosphorylated tau 217 (p-tau217) — a biomarker for Alzheimer’s pathology — found that hormone therapy used for a limited time around menopause is unlikely to increase dementia risk. The study, led by Aladdin Shadyab, adds neurological safety data to the growing evidence base for early HRT initiation.
Why it matters: Dementia fear is one of the biggest barriers to HRT uptake. This biomarker-based study — measuring actual tau pathology rather than relying on clinical diagnosis — provides the most direct evidence yet that timing matters more than the therapy itself.

Chile’s Monte Verde Site Re-Dated — Upending 50 Years of Human Migration Theory
For nearly five decades, Monte Verde in southern Chile was the anchor evidence that humans reached South America before the Clovis people crossed into North America ~13,000 years ago. A team led by Dr Todd Surovell (University of Wyoming) has now shown the site was misdated due to soil erosion pushing recent artefacts into older geological layers. The site is likely only 6,000–8,000 years old — less than half the previous estimate. The study, published in Science, puts the north-to-south expansion theory back at the centre of a fierce archaeological debate.
The dinner-table version: The most famous archaeological site in the Americas was apparently misdated for 50 years because dirt moved downhill — and now the entire theory of how humans colonised the New World is back in play.

GUT MICROBIOME
Mendelian Randomisation Study Identifies Causal Links Between Gut Microbiota and Childhood Obesity
A new study in Nature’s Pediatric Research used Mendelian randomisation — a method that uses genetic variants as instruments to infer causation rather than mere correlation — to investigate the relationship between specific gut microbiota compositions and childhood obesity. The approach sidesteps the confounding that plagues observational microbiome studies. Results identify specific bacterial taxa with causal effects on childhood weight.
Why it matters: Mendelian randomisation is the gold standard for establishing causation without an RCT. This means specific gut bacteria aren’t just correlated with childhood weight — they’re driving it. Targeted probiotic or dietary interventions could follow.
🎯 Why this matters to you With a 5-year-old and a 2-year-old, the gut microbiome window is still wide open for both boys. This study gives you specific taxa to look for — and as a geneticist, you’ll appreciate the Mendelian randomisation methodology over the usual observational hand-waving.
