Eye-Tracking & Scanning Patterns

Nielsen Norman Group's 13-year eye-tracking research program (2006-2024) reveals that 79% of web users scan rather than read. This isn't laziness ‚Äî it's efficiency. Users have learned that scanning delivers nearly the same information value as reading, with dramatically less effort.

The Four Scanning Patterns

1. F-Pattern (Worst)

Users read horizontally across the top, then a shorter horizontal line below, then scan vertically down the left side. Occurs with unstructured text ‚Äî no subheadings, no visual breaks. Trawly avoids this through its strong structural elements.

2. Spotted Pattern

Users fixate on visually distinct elements: bold text, links, coloured words, bulleted lists. Format D's tweet cards, engagement metrics, and bold names naturally trigger this pattern.

3. Layer-Cake Pattern ‚≠ê (Best for Trawly)

üéØ Key Finding
The layer-cake pattern ‚Äî where readers scan headings/subheadings, then read body text only under headings that interest them ‚Äî is the most effective scanning pattern short of reading everything. Format D's category pills + headlines create a perfect layer-cake structure.

In eyetracking heatmaps, the layer-cake pattern appears as horizontal stripes (headings) with blank spaces between them. Trawly's design ‚Äî coloured category pills, bold headlines, tweet cards ‚Äî is textbook layer-cake optimisation.

4. Commitment Pattern (Full Reading)

Full engagement with all content. Only triggered when readers are highly motivated or find content personally relevant. Trawly's "Why this matters to you" boxes are specifically designed to trigger this pattern for individual stories.

Miller's Law: The Magic Number 7¬±2

üìä The Math
14 stories √∑ 6 categories = ~2.3 stories per category. Six categories falls within the 5-9 range of working memory. Each category chunk (1 hero + 1-2 companions) is easily processable. Verdict: 14 stories in 6 categories is cognitively sound ‚Äî specifically because of the chunking structure.

Decision Fatigue

Baumeister's research (2011) shows that decisions drain a finite mental resource. Each story presents micro-decisions: Should I read this? Is this relevant? Should I save/share?

Format D's countermeasures are strong:

  • Category pills pre-filter relevance
  • "Why this matters" boxes answer "is this relevant?" proactively
  • "Skip This" explicitly eliminates a decision
  • Engagement metrics provide social proof, reducing evaluation effort

The Zeigarnik Effect

People remember uncompleted tasks better than completed ones (Zeigarnik, 1927). The brain creates tension around unresolved information. Trawly's teaser paragraph opens multiple Zeigarnik loops simultaneously ‚Äî "a solo dev making $2M/year with no employees, a real estate agent who 3x'd closings with AI..." ‚Äî creating irresistible pull to continue reading.

The Psychology of Virality: Share vs Save vs Like

Berger & Milkman's Framework (2012)

The seminal paper "What Makes Online Content Viral?" (Journal of Marketing Research) analysed every New York Times article over three months:

üìö Key Findings
1. Positive content is more viral than negative
2. Physiological arousal drives sharing ‚Äî high-arousal emotions (awe, anxiety) beat low-arousal (sadness)
3. Practical utility increases virality independently
4. Interestingness and surprise drive sharing independently of emotion

Newsletter Format Analysis: The Top 1%

Stratechery ‚Äî Ben Thompson (~$6-7M ARR)

Single long-form essay, 2,000-4,000 words. Extremely minimal design ‚Äî plain text with occasional links. The content IS the product. Thompson coined terms ("aggregation theory") that became industry standard.

Lesson: Depth of analysis creates irreplaceable value. For Trawly, the equivalent depth comes from synthesis across many sources.

Money Stuff ‚Äî Matt Levine (Bloomberg)

4-6 stories daily, conversational analysis with heavy humour and running jokes. Literally no formatting beyond paragraph breaks. Matt Levine's voice is the product.

üî• Critical Insight
Voice is the ultimate moat. Format matters, but a distinctive editorial voice creates the emotional connection that turns readers into evangelists. Trawly's "Chop" voice is this ‚Äî lean into it harder. This cannot be commoditised by competitors.

Morning Brew (Acquired for $75M)

5-7 stories, casual/witty tone, puns in headlines, emoji use. The "smart friend who reads everything" positioning. Lesson: Consistent structure reduces cognitive load ‚Äî readers learn the format and navigate faster each day (schema theory).

Daily Habit Formation

Nir Eyal's Hooked Model (2014)

Phase 1

Trigger (External ‚Üí Internal)

External: Email notification, inbox presence, consistent send time.
Internal: "I wonder what happened today" ‚Üí reaches for Trawly.
Transition: After ~66 days (Lally et al., 2010, UCL), behaviour becomes automatic.

Phase 2

Action (Minimal Effort)

BJ Fogg's model: Behaviour = Motivation √ó Ability √ó Trigger. Email's advantage: appears in a tool people already open 77 times/day. No login, no app download, no paywall friction.

Phase 3

Variable Reward

Three types (after Skinner): rewards of the tribe (what others bookmarked), the hunt (what happened today?), and the self (mastery ‚Äî "I understand this now"). Different stories with consistent structure = predictable format, unpredictable content.

Phase 4

Investment

Reader replies, shares stories, provides feedback. Investment increases return probability. The reply CTA and feedback prompts serve this function.

Case Studies

Morning Brew: Consistent 6 AM delivery became part of morning routine. Referral program created social commitment.

Wordle: One puzzle per day (scarcity), same time, shareable results, ~3 minutes (predictable commitment).

The Skimm: Running jokes, same structure daily, designed for the "getting ready" routine.

Typography, Colour & Visual Design

The Serif Question

Review of 72 studies (Lund, 1999) found no meaningful difference in reading speed or comprehension between serif and sans-serif on screen. But there are psychological differences: serif signals authority, tradition, editorial quality. Sans-serif signals modernity and technology.

üìù Typography Verdict
Format D's choice of Georgia for body text is excellent ‚Äî it signals editorial authority and "curated publication" rather than "tech product." The sans-serif system font for UI elements creates effective contrast between editorial content and navigation.

Colour System

Format D's warm cream background (#FAF8F5) reduces eye strain vs pure white. Dark text (#1a1a2e) provides strong contrast. Category-specific colours aid rapid scanning (Gestalt similarity principle). The amber accent (#F59E0B) adds energy without overwhelming.

The Psychology of Curation vs Aggregation

Research reveals a fundamental trust asymmetry: algorithm awareness reduces perceived content quality (Eslami et al., 2015). When people know an algorithm chose content, they evaluate it more sceptically.

Human curation implies accountability. A curator has reputation at stake. Readers develop parasocial relationships (Horton & Wohl, 1956) ‚Äî one-sided bonds that create loyalty algorithms can't replicate.

‚≠ê Trawly's Moat
Trawly operates at both curation AND creation ‚Äî it selects stories (curation) and writes original analysis (creation). The "Skip This" section is perhaps its most psychologically powerful element: negative selection builds trust by showing what was deliberately excluded. "If they filtered OUT this popular topic, their positive selections must be genuinely good."
