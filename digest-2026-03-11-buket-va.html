🔥 The Big One
FDA Removes the Black Box Warning From HRT — 20 Years of Fear, Reversed
The FDA has officially removed the "black box" — its highest-severity drug warning — from six menopausal hormone therapies. That warning has been on every HRT prescription since 2002, when a flawed WHI study frightened an entire generation of women (and their doctors) away from treatment. The FDA now says: for women under 60, or within 10 years of menopause onset, benefits far outweigh risks. Twenty-three years of fear, reversed by the same body that created it.
📰 BioPharma Dive / CancerNetwork / Managed Healthcare Executive — Feb 12–13, 2026
The FDA's decision covers Bijuva, Annovera, Femring, Intrarosa, Osphena, and Prefest — removing breast cancer and cardiovascular disease warnings that have shaped clinical practice (and patient fear) for over two decades. The agency's review found prior risk framing was disproportionate and not consistent with current evidence on benefits for recently menopausal women.
Read on BioPharma Dive →
Why it matters: This is the largest single shift in women's hormonal healthcare in two decades. GPs who have been reluctant to prescribe HRT now have the FDA's explicit position behind them. The clinical conversation just changed.
🎯 Why this matters to you You're in early perimenopause awareness — which means you're in exactly the window the FDA is now protecting: under 60, within 10 years of onset. The decisions you make in the next 2–3 years about HRT will have compound effects on your cardiovascular, cognitive, and bone health for decades. Go to your GP armed with this. The old "it causes breast cancer" line is now formally off the table.

🃏 Wildcard
Outside your usual read. Intellectually worth 2 minutes.
40,000-Year-Old Symbols May Be the Oldest Writing System Ever Discovered
Archaeologists Eva Dutkiewicz and Christian Bentz studied 260 Palaeolithic artefacts from caves in Germany — small objects of bone, antler, and ivory scratched with surprisingly complex symbols by Homo sapiens hunter-gatherers around 38,000–40,000 BCE. The symbols show the same statistical complexity as the earliest Mesopotamian proto-cuneiform from 3,500 BCE. They weren't drawings. They weren't random. They were a structured communication system — encoding meaning that is now irrecoverably lost. Writing didn't appear from nowhere in ancient Mesopotamia. It had a 36,000-year prehistory we're only beginning to see.
The dinner-table version: "The oldest writing ever found is 40,000 years old — scratched into bone by people who hadn't invented agriculture yet — and we still have no idea what it says."
