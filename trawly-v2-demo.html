The Big One
Levels: Big Tech AI Is Coming for Every Niche — and You've Got Less Time Than You Think
Pieter Levels posted the most-bookmarked thing in AI yesterday. His argument: Custom GPTs launched in 2023 and were dead within a year because general models got better at everything faster than any niche player could. That cycle is now repeating for the entire app economy.
"General models will keep getting better at everything — sucking in more knowledge. I think slowly Big Tech will suck up all apps. Before they couldn't do niches and we could. But with AI they can do niches faster than we ever could." — @levelsio, 97 bookmarks
If general AI can clone any niche app in weeks, the moat isn't features anymore — it's distribution, brand, and relationships. The window to own your category is narrower than it's ever been.
For Trawly specifically: no general model ships a product that knows your 5-year-old has sleep apnea and that you'd rather have cold calling hacks than headlines. The window is closing for undifferentiated tools. It's wide open for things that actually know you.

Builders & Founders
ATMs Didn't Kill Bank Teller Jobs — But the iPhone Did. What That Tells Us About AI.
David Oks published an essay that should be mandatory reading for anyone building against AI disruption. ATM numbers went UP after ATMs because automation cut costs per branch, so banks opened more branches. But branch numbers collapsed when mobile banking made physical branches irrelevant. The distinction: automation expands demand; substitution eliminates it. AI is doing both simultaneously, at different speeds, in different industries.
Enviz sits at an interesting intersection here — you're not making display suites more efficient, you're making them irrelevant. That's not ATM-style (more efficient); that's iPhone-style (the old model stops making sense). Worth putting in your next developer pitch exactly that way.

Stanford: Memory Loss Isn't Inevitable. It's a Gut Problem We Can Fix.
Dr. Christoph Thaiss at Stanford found that cognitive decline in aging mice is directly linked to changes in gut bacteria that impair the vagus nerve — the gut-brain highway. When they restored that connection, old mice formed memories like young ones again. "The timeline of memory decline is not hardwired; it's actively modulated in the body."
Research is in mice, but the mechanism — vagus nerve, gut bacteria composition — applies directly to human biology. This points to dietary and microbiome interventions as the lever, not drugs. One of the most significant brain-health findings in recent years.
