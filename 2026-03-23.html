Quantum Cryptography Just Won the Turing Award — And the Hack-Proof Encryption It Invented Is Now Everywhere
Charles Bennett (IBM, 50+ years) and Gilles Brassard just won the 2026 A.M. Turing Award — computing’s Nobel Prize — for inventing BB84, the foundation of quantum key cryptography. Their insight: any attempt to intercept a quantum encryption key physically changes its behaviour, making eavesdropping mathematically impossible to hide. The protocol they wrote in 1984 on a napkin now secures financial systems, military comms, and post-quantum encryption standards worldwide.
The dinner-table version: Two physicists figured out in 1984 that quantum particles can’t be secretly copied — observation changes them. They turned that physics quirk into an encryption system that is literally impossible to hack without leaving evidence. It took 40 years for the world to catch up with what they wrote on a napkin.
