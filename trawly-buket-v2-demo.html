The Big One
The FDA just removed HRT's black box warning. All of it. Breast cancer, heart disease, dementia — gone.
On February 12, 2026, the FDA formally removed the black box warnings from all four categories of hormone replacement therapy — the first major reversal since the 2002 Women's Health Initiative study triggered 23 years of fear. The updated labelling covers systemic estrogen alone, estrogen plus progesterone, systemic progestogen-alone therapy, and topical vaginal estrogen.
"This decision reflects our commitment to follow the science wherever it leads and to correct course when the evidence demands it." — FDA Commissioner, February 2026
The WHI's black box warnings suppressed HRT prescribing for two decades — GPs stopped recommending it and women suffered unnecessarily. This is a formal regulatory acknowledgment that the original risk calculus was wrong, particularly for women who initiate HRT early in the perimenopause-to-menopause transition.
You already know the WHI reanalysis. But now the regulatory framework has caught up. If your GP was hesitant because of the black box, that barrier is gone. You're in the right window — early perimenopause, 41–45 peak symptom band — to have this conversation with updated evidence firmly behind you.

Women's Health
Pharmacies can't keep estrogen patches on shelves. The demand surge is real.
Since the FDA removed the black box warning, prescriptions for transdermal estrogen have surged so dramatically that major chains like CVS are reporting rolling shortages. Amneal Pharmaceuticals — one of the main manufacturers of estradiol patches — confirmed to NPR that they've seen a "significant increase in demand" and are actively ramping up production.
The shortage is a supply signal. Millions of women were waiting for this — demand was suppressed by stigma and clinical hesitancy, not by actual lack of need. Access is now the limiting factor, not safety.

Perimenopause can start in your early 30s — and more than 90% of affected women eventually seek medical care
A new analysis in Contemporary OB/GYN confirms that perimenopause symptoms are common from the early 30s, peak between 41–45, and decline after 56. Physical symptoms (vaginal dryness, bladder issues, sexual function) become predominant after 51 while vasomotor symptoms peak 51–55. The researchers explicitly call for earlier clinical awareness and intervention — a direct challenge to GPs who wait for dramatic symptom presentations before engaging.
This is peer-reviewed backing for what you're already living. You're squarely in the 41–45 peak band. The 90% medical-care statistic is a useful data point for any clinician who implies you're ahead of schedule — you're not. You're exactly on time.

Cancer Genetics
BC Cancer just moved Optical Genome Mapping to first-tier status for myeloid malignancies — effective immediately
BC Cancer's Cancer Genetics & Genomics Lab has formally made OGM the first-line test for AML, MDS, and other myeloid malignancies — immediately following a pilot study that proved it catches clinically significant chromosomal abnormalities that conventional karyotyping and FISH miss. This is not a research protocol. It's a live clinical decision affecting patients right now.
OGM has been promising in research contexts for years — now a major clinical lab is treating it as standard of care. This is the proof-of-concept other labs have been waiting for before committing to workflow changes. Worth flagging with your team if you haven't already.
Columbia's clinical cytogenetics director elected President of the ACMG Foundation
Dr. Brynn Levy — Director of Clinical Cytogenetics at Columbia and one of the most cited voices in the karyotype-to-molecular-cytogenomics transition — has been elected President of the ACMG Foundation. The ACMG Foundation shapes clinical genetics practice guidelines globally. Levy's specific focus on OGM transition means formal guidance accelerating the technology's adoption is more likely under his tenure.
ACMG guidelines shape what clinical labs are expected to offer. The right person in this role at the right moment for the field.
Waiv (formerly Owkin Dx) raises $33M to push AI cancer diagnostics into clinical deployment
Waiv — the renamed diagnostics spinout from French AI-pharma company Owkin — has secured $33M from five investors. Unlike most of the AI oncology space (which focuses on drug discovery), Waiv is building AI-powered pathology tools aimed directly at the diagnostic end: how cancers are detected, classified, and staged at the lab level.
AI-powered pathology is moving from research to clinical deployment faster than most expected. This is capital pointed at changing how cancers are classified and detected at the lab bench. Your field in 3–5 years.
Outside your usual net
Scientists found traces of life pressed into Morocco's mountain rock — where no life should have survived
Geologists trekking through Morocco's Atlas Mountains found mysterious wrinkle patterns in ancient sedimentary rock at high altitude — sediments that were once deep-sea ocean floor. After ruling out every other explanation, researchers concluded the patterns were left by chemosynthetic microbial mats: colonies of microbes that lived in total, permanent darkness, surviving purely on chemical energy. Millions of years of tectonic movement lifted the ocean floor thousands of metres into the sky, but the microbial signatures survived the entire journey.
Morocco's mountains used to be ocean floor. The wrinkles in the rock are microbes from the deep dark. They left their mark before the continents finished moving.
Alternative Education
Classic Montessori vs the adapted versions — the outcome gap is real, and it comes down to one word: adherence
Programs that stick rigorously to the original method — phonics-based literacy, sensory-based mathematics, multi-age classrooms, uninterrupted 3-hour work periods — produce substantially better outcomes than "supplemented" Montessori programs that blend in conventional schooling. The Montessori brand has been heavily diluted. Most programs using the name don't follow the method.
Before any school tour, ask specifically: AMI-accredited? 3-hour uninterrupted work cycles? Mixed ages 3–6 in the same classroom? The school's answers will tell you more than the brochure. You're choosing for your 5yo — these are the three questions that matter.
Child Health
What a mother eats during pregnancy changes her baby's gut microbiome from week one — and the immune system pays the price
Mother mice fed common food emulsifiers — polysorbate 80, carrageenan, and related compounds found in nearly all processed and packaged foods — had offspring whose gut microbiome was measurably altered from the first weeks of life. These changes interfered with normal immune system training in the gut, leading to long-term chronic inflammation and increased susceptibility to inflammatory conditions as adults.
Your boys are 2 and 5 — the window for microbiome shaping is still very open, especially for your 2-year-old. This isn't about perfect eating; it's a signal to keep processed emulsifier-heavy foods low in their diet. The gut diversity built in the first 3 years has outsized long-term effects on immune regulation.

Medicinal Gardening
The clinical evidence for ashwagandha has caught up to what Ayurvedic herbalists said for centuries
Multiple RCTs now show significant reductions in cortisol levels, anxiety scores, and self-reported chronic stress. The HPA-axis modulation mechanism is reasonably well characterised. Ashwagandha leads the adaptogen evidence base — well ahead of rhodiola, holy basil, and others commonly marketed alongside it. It's a semi-tropical perennial that handles Sydney's coastal climate well.
If you're building a medicinal garden for functional benefit — HPA axis, stress regulation, cortisol — this is the highest-evidence adaptogen you can grow at home. The cortisol-HRT connection is also worth exploring given the perimenopause context.
Basil isn't just a kitchen herb — researchers are taking its bioactive compounds seriously in longevity research
Eugenol and rosmarinic acid in sweet basil have demonstrated anti-inflammatory activity in multiple studies. Linalool has anxiolytic effects in animal models. Longevity researchers are flagging basil as a genuine contributor to the anti-inflammatory properties of the Mediterranean diet — distinct from other herbs in that diet, not just a flavour component. It grows year-round in Sydney and is one of the easiest garden herbs to maintain.
If you're building a medicinal garden with functional intent, it earns its place on bioactivity grounds — particularly the anti-inflammatory angle, which pairs directly with the perimenopause-inflammation connection.

The gut microbiome may explain why humans got big brains — and what you feed your kids might matter more than you think
Northwestern University researchers transplanted gut microbes from primates with large brains (humans and squirrel monkeys) and small brains (macaques) into germ-free mice. After 8 weeks, mice that received large-brain primate microbes showed clearly different brain activity patterns — higher expression of genes linked to energy production and synaptic plasticity, the process underlying learning and memory. Prof. Katie Amato calls this the first direct experimental evidence that the gut microbiome shapes brain function across primate species.
"Our study shows that microbes are acting on traits that are relevant to our understanding of evolution, and particularly the evolution of human brains." — Prof. Katie Amato, Northwestern University, January 2026
This ties directly to everything else in today's issue — the emulsifier study (microbiome altered from birth), the gut bacteria sensing paper (microbiome actively reading its environment), and the adaptogen research (feeding the gut-brain axis). If you only read one thing in depth today, this is the thread that connects all of it.
