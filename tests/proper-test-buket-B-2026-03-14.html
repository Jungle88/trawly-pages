WOMEN’S HEALTH
NHS Approves Veoza: First Non-Hormonal Menopause Drug for Hot Flushes
The NHS has approved Veoza (fezolinetant), a non-hormonal treatment targeting vasomotor symptoms in menopause. It works by blocking neurokinin 3 receptors in the hypothalamus — directly targeting the temperature dysregulation pathway rather than replacing oestrogen. For women who can’t or won’t take HRT, this is the first evidence-based pharmaceutical alternative with solid trial data. Another drug, elinzanetant, is also moving through approval for both hot flushes and insomnia.
Why it matters: This is the first time in decades we have a genuinely new pharmacological option for menopausal symptoms that isn’t HRT. For breast cancer survivors or women with contraindications, this is significant news.

Excessive Screen Time Before Age 5 Slows White-Matter Pathway Development
New MRI research shows children with excessive screen time before age five have slower growth of white-matter pathways — the neural tracts essential for language, literacy, and cognitive processing. The study used diffusion tensor imaging to track myelination patterns over time. The effect was dose-dependent: more screen time correlated with slower white matter maturation. These pathways are foundational for reading readiness and verbal reasoning.

The Franklin Expedition Ships Were Found After 170 Years — Here’s What We Now Know
Sir John Franklin and his crew set sail on May 19, 1845, heading for treacherous Arctic seas. At the three-year mark of the voyage, no one back home heard from the two ships, Erebus and Terror — and they wouldn’t be seen again for nearly 170 years. The ships were finally discovered in 2014 and 2016, remarkably preserved in the cold Arctic waters. Archaeologists have now retrieved over 350 artefacts including crew belongings, ship’s plates, and personal items. The mystery of what happened to the 129 men is slowly being pieced together: lead poisoning, scurvy, and ultimately desperation as supplies ran out.
The dinner-table version: Two Victorian ships vanished in the Arctic. 170 years later, we found them perfectly preserved. The artefacts are rewriting what we know about one of history’s most famous disasters.

New Immunotherapy Shows Up to 99% Drop in Prostate Cancer Biomarkers
Researchers tested a new immunotherapy drug called VIR-5500 in men with advanced metastatic prostate cancer. The treatment is a PSMA targeting T-cell engager, designed to link immune T-cells directly to cancer cells expressing PSMA. Early results showed up to 99% reduction in PSA levels in some patients. This is still early-stage, but the mechanism — bispecific T-cell engagement targeting PSMA — represents a new class of prostate cancer treatment.
Why it matters: T-cell engagers are one of the most exciting areas in immuno-oncology. Prostate cancer has been historically difficult to treat with immunotherapy — this approach may change that.

🧵 Thread of the Day
Estrogen During the Follicular Phase: Why Your Brain Is “Fertilised” for Learning
A neuroscience thread explaining why the follicular phase of the menstrual cycle is optimal for learning new things. Estrogen peaks during this phase and directly upregulates BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), which acts like “fertiliser for new synaptic connections.” Long-term potentiation — the neural basis of memory formation — is measurably easier during this window. Your brain is literally primed for learning in the first half of your cycle.
